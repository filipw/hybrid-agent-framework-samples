---

**Max Planck**, in 1900, introduced the quantum hypothesis, proposing that the energy emitted or absorbed by atoms is not continuous, but comes in discrete packets called "quanta." This foundational idea marked the birth of quantum theory.

**Albert Einstein**, in 1905, extended Planck’s hypothesis by proposing that light itself consists of quanta (later called photons). He used this idea to explain the photoelectric effect, demonstrating that energy transfer in light also occurs in discrete amounts, further confirming the quantum nature of phenomena.

**Niels Bohr** built on these ideas in 1913 by proposing a new atomic model in which electrons travel in specific, quantized orbits around the nucleus. His model explained why atoms emit or absorb light at only certain wavelengths, successfully accounting for the spectral lines of hydrogen.

---

Together, their contributions established the fundamental principles of quantum mechanics: the quantization of energy, the particle nature of light, and the quantum structure of atoms.
